Scope

The scope of this project included a full website redesign with the intent of driving up ticket sales, implementing a new look, and making the website mobile responsive. The team ensured that the website met state guidelines for accessibility and functionality, which included meeting WCAG 2.1 Level AA guidelines. We also will be implementing a Spanish version of the site in a future version which will be reflected in the design.

Constraints

The team I worked with had a bit over a three month timeline for this project to implement the full UX process and development of a WordPress custom theme; we launched at the end of January 2020.

Problem

The organization felt that their website’s content was robust but was lacking clear organization and wanted their website to be redesigned so that their audiences could easily find what they needed -- driving ticket sales and memberships up in the process. Austin Opera also felt that their website was not on point with their current direction and needed a revamped look and feel.

I found that the navigation structure was confusing to users and they were unsure where to find important information. The homepage was missing an oppurtunity to direct users where the organization wanted them to go. I also found that the website was content dense and lacking the visual hierarchy cues needed to guide users. Lastly, I found that users wanted more meaningful and impactful imagery to enrich their overall experience.

Solution

I worked with users to design a website that addressed the pain points discovered during research.The navigation system was restructured to redefine existing information hierarchy and was tested with users to ensure usability. Visual hierarchy cues such as distinct sections, headers, and bright call to actions were added to enhance the website’s usability, providing a visual guide to help the user discover useful information. The website was redesigned to allow for more images to be implemented providing users with a visually compelling experience.

Discovery

Research

I started by doing a competitive analysis of other performing arts organizations both local to Austin and in other cities in Texas to see what other organizations were doing well in comparison. Then, we completed a heuristic evaluation of the current website to discover areas for improvement.

After learning about the typical demographics through interviews with screened users, I tested my assumptions of the current website by leading the users through a series of scenarios. Based on the feedback from the users, their opinions closely resembled mine.

“It would be nice to see some pictures and a much smaller explanation of what the production is, I don't want to read all of this.”



“Things aren't really labeled clearly or they aren't put in categories that I would expect them to be in.”

Key Takeaways

1. Users desired a more compelling experience. Being that performing art patrons are fans because they liked to escape reality, they expected the website to have a similar effect.


2. They were frustrated with the navigation menu and found that they had a hard time finding specific information.


3. Users wanted an easier way to access the calendar as it was hidden inside of a mega menu and wasn’t easy to find.


4. Users found that there was too much text and they did not want to read through it.

User Persona

The opera goers we spoke to were male and female, in their mid twenties to mid forties, and had been fans of the of the performing arts since they were young. They had goals like discovering new productions, choose a production to see, and to discover vistor details.

Information Architecture

Cardsort

Being that users were frustrated with the website’s architecture, I did an open cart sort to discover new ways to organize the website’s information. My team sorted the cards internally and came up with similiar results. I used the final sort to inform the sitemap

Information Architecture

I completed a content inventory of the current site to record the varying pages of the website and to allow the client to decide which pages should be displayed in the new website. I figured out a better way to organize content so the end user could discover information, but still needed to build things in a way so the stakeholder could easily manage information.

I got with my team to brainstorm information architecture ideas on the whiteboard, exploring ways the client could interact with the WP dashboard. We decided that performances, events, locations, staff, sponsors, opportunities (auditions and employment) would have unique post types and there would be taxonomies for season, events, and types of education.

User Flow

A user flow diagram was created to note the complete path a user would take when completing tasks on the website. Being that there are numerous paths a user could take based on their interests therefore, the most important flows were selected and diagrammed. These flows were chosen based on the website’s main goals which were defined during client meetings and confirmed through user research focusing on:


1. Discovering performances and buying tickets

2. Planning a visit to the opera

3. Supporting the organization

Usability Testing

I tested the UX Wireframes with users to verify design decisions. Due to budget constraints, I chose to Guerilla test the clickable prototype on users of varying demographics.

Iterations based on testing results

Problem

Users did not use the donate button to complete the task, so the donate button was removed and replaced with the Spanish version toggle.

Solution

It was found that using a Spanish translation icon was not as effective as having a clearly labeled button that said, En Espanol.

Problem

Users were uncertain what the icons meant, especially in this version as icon placeholders were used instead of unique iconography.

Solution

Icon labels were added to reduce friction.

Problem

Users thought Support in the main navigation menu was a place to ask for help. They eventually realized that Support was a place to contribute to the organization.

Solution

To reduce friction Support was changed to Support Us.

Visual Design

Style Tiles

During the kick off meeting, I discovered brand adjectives the client thought best represented their brand. The budget allowed for two styles tiles to be designed, and I used their brand adjectives to influenced the two styles. The client had an internal review to vote on their favorite, and this was the style selected.

The selected style influenced the look and feel of the high fidelity mockups.

High Fidelity Mockups

I designed high fidelity versions of the landing page, single page, and single post type using Figma. I went through a couple of rounds of preference testing to ensure the styling of the landing page work for the end user and the client.


Final Stages

Development & QA

The developer built the website on WordPress with a custom theme to give the client more control. After the development release, I did several rounds of internal QA testing to ensure functionality. After the site was fully developed, tested and deployed, Ci worked with the client to train them how to use the custom theme.

Results

I created a user experience allowing for the intended audiences to easily navigate Austin Opera’s website to find what they need. The designed solution included vivid imagery, scannability, and visual appeal. The design was tested to ensure that users were leaving feeling delighted, rather than frustrated and confused. The team helped me integrated multiple interactive visual and social media components.

During custom theme development in Wordpress, my team ensured that the management of events was easier for the admins by creating a system so events automatically move to archive, eliminating the need for manual removal. The archived events were still viewable and searchable by users.

Being that this website was recently launched, I don't have solid success metrics to share, but am looking forward to adding them one that data is available.

THE LIVE WEBSITE

Conclusion

Being that this was my first client project, I learned a lot about the constraints of having a budget. In the bootcamp I attended or when designing things with friends, I had a lot of time to make things perfect. I learned that perfect does not always define done - and that is okay.

One of the mistakes I made was not updating earlier design artifacts when I iterated on the mockups. Not updating the sitemap and user flow caused some confusion with the developers. Luckily, being an inhouse team, we were able to smooth things over quickly. During this project, I also learned that communication with the developer early on makes for a smoother handoff. With that said, it was fun to iterate my design on the fly to redesign sections that were a bit too complex for our budget constraints. I was glad to learn these lessons to improve my process.
